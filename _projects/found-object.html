Overview

Found Object is an app to collect things found in the wild / in real life (not bits of data) and make them come to life in a curated display. This is a platform that functions as personal archive (where you tag things with metadata) and a personal museum (to curate your found objects). Inspired by the thrill of treasure-hunting, making art out of scraps, and crow gift.

The name comes from the art term: A found object is a natural or man-made object that is found by an artist and kept because of some intrinsic interest the artist sees in it. (Tate).

The Problem Space

Artists and creatives find inspiration in both the digital and analog realms:
e.g., using pen and paper sketches, going to curated exhibitions IRL and online, walking in nature, looking at other artists' work on social media, etc..

The Details

The building process was divided into two parts: the research phase and the designing phase.

During the research phase, I led a group of three other researchers, designating roles and setting a timeline based on our respective abilities and schedules. Our research methods included user surverys, interviews, user personas, and journeymapping.

The designing phase was carried out individually. I conducted ideation sessions, created a design system, and completed a high-fidelity prototype of the final product.

Preliminary User Research

Goals

  1. How do artists find inspiration for their projects?
  2. What tools do they currently use?

Audience

Young creatives who are familiar with digital tools already
= College-aged + Young professionals

I created surveys and interview guide, which my research team then spread to students at various college campuses.

In addition, I conducted a couple of competitive analyses on two inspiration based apps, Pinterest and Are.na

In addition, I conducted a couple of competitive analyses on two inspiration based apps, Pinterest and Are.na.

The key difference between these two apps and my idea, though, is the emphasis on the creating of rather than the sharing of content, and the content has a mapping to the non-digital world (one mostly shares things that have physical instantiation in your own life, like anything you possess or come across on the street).

Key Insight

The key insight from the preliminary research was that young artists and creatives still find inspiration and joy in their non-digital lives.

One interviewee said, "I think [ an ideal tool] would bridge the analog and the digital."

User Testing

With a functional mid-fi prototype (see right), I conducted 7 user tests. The green sticky notes are some of the quoted comments on the design and user flows.

The general feedback spoke to the absence of search functions within each page, incorporating a more interactive curation space, and increasing the flexibility to edit app objects.

Based on UI Feedback: I changed the color scheme to reduce grayscale prominence and the bright red main color (changing it to a maroon).

Reflections

In completing my final design, I realized why so many social media platforms tend to converge to a similar design and user flow.

For example, maybe from pure overexposure, or pure human-centeredness, it makes sense to make the most ego-centric (Profile) on the right and the most intersubjective on the left side (Social Feed).

If I had to guess: for right-handed people the thumb is closest to the right-side of the screen (easy access to the ego :)
