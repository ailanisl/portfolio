Overview

This is a personal wiki and digital florilegium that stores linked articles, quotes, thoughts, music, and art.

I learned HTML, CSS, and Javascript to code this website by hand, as I wanted to greatest degree of latitude to execute my vision, and to keep the site as low-tech and as maintainable as possible.

It's a continual work-in-progress, but please feel free to poke around: The Pocketbook

Problem Space

With social media overrun by corporate interests that exploit our attention and psychological vulnerabilities for profit, I became disillusioned with the Internet as a whole.

I didn't come of age in the nascency of the Internet, but rather during the adolescence of social media. And, I had fun on social media: I loved putting my style and tastes on display, curating art images on the Instagram grid, sharing what I am listening to at the moment.

Yet, it was just the structure of those platforms that felt limiting to creative impulsivity. Everything was so contrived.

But a personal website gives one the latitude to do all of the above without the burden of templates.

I wanted something that supported my personal knowledge database, commonly referred to as a commonplace. I obsessively take notes on things, collect quotes, and log my books in my commonplace notebooks.

The problem was that, being analog, there was no comprehensive index or organizing system. If I wanted to go back to something, I'd have to flip through copious pages and hope my memory was up to the task.

I had a supplemental Google Keep account to store some of my quotes, and I was impressed by the tagging system and how easily it allowed me to synthesize ideas and make connections between disparate disciplines.

Notion, Obsidian, other note-taking applications were existent, but the lack of design freedom put me off. I needed to make something myself.

Solution

A hand-coded website to preserve my commonplace journal. A “pocketbook” that is a knowledge repository and contains my collections of quotes, poems, music, books, in addition to some of my thoughts.

These were pretty common in the early Internet, with websites such as Gwern, Bactra.org, and Piero Scaruffi being popular public knowledge bases.

Now, these type of websites manifest themselves as "digital gardens" which have been pretty popular as of late.

Critical Learning Curve

To do all this, I need to first know how to even start making a website. So, I found a student-taught course (Web Design Decal) through Berkeley, available on Youtube for free.

And so, I learned HTML, CSS, and Javascript in a matter of three weeks.

Research and Design

For the visual design of the website, I was inspired by the old internet style, the hand-coded and ambient web, and minimalist UI. I wanted a low-tech site and to minimize my use of Javascript, so no fancy animations, no web-downloaded fonts. But I did want to include images (dithered and svg wherever possible), so it is an aspiring low-tech site.

I did extensive research (i.e. web-surfing) and mood-boarding to get a sense of what has been done and what is feasible. A collection of simple sites (and their links) I like is available to view on my Are.na (44 blocks at time of writing).

Wireframes and Prototyping

In Figma, I designed hi-fi prototype of the website, with sets of components and their variables.

I went through lots of different versions for minute elements of the web design (e.g., the recent post cards).

I wanted something clean and simple, yet offered some bit of creative unexpectedness.

Frontend Implementation

When I finished the designs I was at a lost as to how to convert those to HTML and CSS.

So I meandered over to Webflow, hand-copied my Figma designs in there. The structure that Webflow forces you to use actually made me visualize how I was supposed to copy my prototypes into HTML/CSS.

ChatGPT and AI tools were helpful in getting me unstuck for coding problems, but I limited my use, as I didn't want to rely on it too much.

I decided to use Jekyll as a static site generator, and Github to host it. Thanks to Giraffe Academy on Youtube I learned how to navigate the jungle of Jekyll.

I learned a lot about the Liquid (template), markdown, and YAML (data serialization) languages just by doing in the process.
